Historically, exponential notation \(a^x\) was used for natural number exponents. Euclid used the term “power” to mean the square of a line segment, and Archimedes used the idea of powers to work with base 10 arithmetic. Radical notation \(\sqrt[q]{a}\) used for square roots, cube roots, etc. Eventually, some mathematicians realized they could unify the notation, using power properties. A \(q\)-th root of \(a\text{,}\) by definition, is a solution to \(x^q=a\text{.}\) Therefore, applying power properties to the notation \(a^{1/q}\) yields \((a^{1/q})^q=a^{\frac{1}{q}\cdot q}=a^1 =a\text{.}\)
