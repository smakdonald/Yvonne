Item 1.7.8.b.
A third student says: “Wait a minute ... if \(5^\pi=5^{3}5^{0.1}5^{0.04}5^{0.001}5^{0.0005}5^{0.00009}\dots\text{,}\) the exponents keep on getting smaller and smaller, so shouldn't that mean that you're multiplying by smaller and smaller numbers. So each factor \(5^{3}\text{,}\) \(5^{0.1}\text{,}\) \(5^{0.04}\text{,}\) \(5^{0.001}\text{,}\) \(5^{0.0005}\text{,}\) \(5^{0.00009}\) is getting closer and closer to \(0\text{.}\) So then \(5^\pi\) should be really tiny, close to 0.”
A fourth student says: “I disagree, \(5^\pi\) should be larger than \(5^3\text{,}\) which is \(125\text{.}\) That's way bigger than \(0\text{.}\)”
Prepare for responding to the students. In your preparations:
  • Help make sense of why even though \(5^{0.1}\text{,}\) \(5^{0.04}\text{,}\) \(5^{0.001}\text{,}\) \(5^{0.0005}\text{,}\) \(5^{0.00009}\text{,}\) …, are getting smaller and smaller, that they don't necessarily limit to \(0\)
  • Explain what this sequence actually limits to
  • Explain how to make sense of why \(5^{3}5^{0.1}5^{0.04}5^{0.001}5^{0.0005}5^{0.00009}\dots\) is greater than \(125\text{.}\)
