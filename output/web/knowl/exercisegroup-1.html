8.

To approximate \(\pi\text{,}\) we could use its decimal expansion – we take \(3\text{,}\) then \(3.1\text{,}\) then \(3.14\text{,}\) then \(3.141\text{,}\) then \(3.1415\text{,}\) then \(3.14159\text{,}\) etc. So \(\pi\) is the limit of
\begin{equation*} 3,\, 3.1,\, 3.14, \, 3.141, \, 3.1415, \, 3.14159, \dots \end{equation*}
We can do a similar thing with powers! So \(5^\pi\) is the limit of
\begin{equation*} 5^3, \, 5^{3.1},\, 5^{3.14}, \, 5^{3.141}, \, 5^{3.1415}, \, 5^{3.14159}, \dots \end{equation*}
  1. Suppose that you are teaching about real powers, and you use \(5^\pi\) as an example. A student asks you about power properties, and you review them using the example:
    \begin{equation*} 5^{3.1}=5^{3+0.1}=5^{3}5^{0.1}. \end{equation*}
    You ask them to break down \(5^{3.141}\) in a similar fashion.
    A student says: “We could do this forever! \(5^\pi\) is \(5^{3}5^{0.1}5^{0.04}5^{0.001}5^{0.0005}5^{0.00009}\dots\)”
    Another student responds: “How did you get all those 0's? why isn't it just \(5^{3}5^{1}5^{4}5^{1}5^{5}5^{9}\dots\text{?}\)”
    Explain why the first student's work makes sense, and help the second student make sense of why the 0's should be there.
  2. A third student says: “Wait a minute ... if \(5^\pi=5^{3}5^{0.1}5^{0.04}5^{0.001}5^{0.0005}5^{0.00009}\dots\text{,}\) the exponents keep on getting smaller and smaller, so shouldn't that mean that you're multiplying by smaller and smaller numbers. So each factor \(5^{3}\text{,}\) \(5^{0.1}\text{,}\) \(5^{0.04}\text{,}\) \(5^{0.001}\text{,}\) \(5^{0.0005}\text{,}\) \(5^{0.00009}\) is getting closer and closer to \(0\text{.}\) So then \(5^\pi\) should be really tiny, close to 0.”
    A fourth student says: “I disagree, \(5^\pi\) should be larger than \(5^3\text{,}\) which is \(125\text{.}\) That's way bigger than \(0\text{.}\)”
    Prepare for responding to the students. In your preparations:
    • Help make sense of why even though \(5^{0.1}\text{,}\) \(5^{0.04}\text{,}\) \(5^{0.001}\text{,}\) \(5^{0.0005}\text{,}\) \(5^{0.00009}\text{,}\) …, are getting smaller and smaller, that they don't necessarily limit to \(0\)
    • Explain what this sequence actually limits to
    • Explain how to make sense of why \(5^{3}5^{0.1}5^{0.04}5^{0.001}5^{0.0005}5^{0.00009}\dots\) is greater than \(125\text{.}\)
